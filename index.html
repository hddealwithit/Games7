William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) was an English playwright, poet, and actor. He is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and one of the most influential literary figures in world history.

Born in Stratford-upon-Avon, he was baptized on 26 April 1564 and grew up in a market town shaped by Tudor politics, religion, and trade. He likely attended the King's New School, where students studied Latin grammar, rhetoric, and classical authors such as Ovid, Seneca, and Virgil—texts that later echoed throughout his writing.

By the late 1580s or early 1590s, Shakespeare moved to London, where he worked as an actor and playwright. He eventually became a shareholder in the Lord Chamberlain's Men (later the King's Men), giving him both artistic and financial influence in one of the city's most successful theater companies.

Major Works and Genres

Shakespeare's surviving body of work includes about 39 plays, 154 sonnets, and several longer poems. His comedies—such as A Midsummer Night's Dream, As You Like It, and Twelfth Night—blend romance, clever wordplay, and mistaken identity. His histories—like Richard II, Henry IV, and Richard III—dramatize royal power, civil conflict, and national identity.

His tragedies, including Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, and Macbeth, probe ambition, jealousy, betrayal, grief, and moral responsibility. These plays are admired for psychological complexity and enduring questions about justice, conscience, and human frailty.

Poetry and Lasting Legacy

Shakespeare's sonnets remain central to English lyric poetry. They explore time, love, beauty, memory, mortality, and artistic permanence with compact structure and striking imagery. Sonnet 18 ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?") and Sonnet 116 ("Let me not to the marriage of true minds") are among the most quoted poems in English.

His company performed at major venues including the Globe Theatre and Blackfriars Theatre, helping spread his work during his lifetime. Over four centuries later, Shakespeare's plays are still staged globally, adapted into films, and taught in classrooms across continents. Everyday English still carries his imprint through phrases like "break the ice," "wild-goose chase," and "heart of gold."
